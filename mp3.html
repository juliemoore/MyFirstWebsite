Impact of the MP3 File

MP3 technology was developed by a team of engineer at Fraunhofer IIs, University of Hanover, AT&T-Bell Labs, Thomson-Brandt, CCETT and many others, but the most recognized name is Karlheinz Brandenburg, “The Father of MP3.” (Rose, 2011) MP3 (MPEG Layer III) is a standard for audio file compression that reduces the size of the file without a loss in the sound quality using lossy compression technology. MP3 is a part of the Motion Pictures Expert Group (MPEG) that defines the standards for displaying video and audio. These standards were set beginning in 1991 by the MPEG subcommittee of the Industry Standards Organization/ International Electrotechnical Commission (ISO/IEC) with the MPEG-1, a video compression standard followed by the MPEG-2, an audio and video file compression standard used in DVD technology. (Bellis, n.d.) MPEG continues to set standards with the addition of the multimedia MP4 standard. In 1998, the ISO/IEC published a reference software in C language that simulated the hardware d ecoding of compressed audio.

In 1994, the Fraunhofer Society released the first MP3 encoder and named the filename extension .mp3. On September 9, 1995, the first software MP3 player was released and people can begin to play MP3 files on their PCs. This technology was essential for PC users at the time because hard drives weren’t large enough to store large audio files, such as music, without compression technology. (MP3, n.d.)

The rise of the internet in the 90’s also led to the rise of widespread distribution of MP3 files over the internet. Owners of copyrighted music CDs were now able to “rip” songs into a MP3 file format and share music with their peers. Peer-to-peer free file sharing networks, such as Napster, became popular at the turn of the century. This led to major record companies filing lawsuits against Napster and other individuals for copyright infringement. (Lalwani, 2015) This did little to stop the practice and is more widespread with the emergence of YouTube and other social media websites. Essentially, the MP3 file format dismantled the music industry, gave rise to new industries and changed the way we listen to music.

MP3 technology transformed the consumer electronic industry and music consumers by providing a compact audio storage format that allows for a handheld portable device to store hundreds to thousands of songs. The iPod, launched in 2001, is the most notable MP3 player. (Turner, 2011)
